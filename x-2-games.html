15 2 x 2 games

15.1 Instructions

Rounds and Matchings: The experiment consists of a number of rounds. Note: In each round, you will be matched with another person selected at random from the other participants. There will be a new random matching each round. The number of rounds in this part of the experiment is: 8.

Interdependence: Your earnings are determined by the decisions that you and the other person make.

Roles: In each pair of people, one person will be designated as the “row” player and the other will be the “column” player. You will be a row player in all rounds.

As a row player, you will press either the Top or the Bottom button. The column player will choose Left or Right. These choices determine which part of the matrix is relevant (Top Left, Top Right, Bottom Left, Bottom Right). In each cell, your payoff is shown in blue and the column player’s payoff is shown in red. Payoff Matrix (Row, Column) Left Right $3.00, $3.00 $0.00, $5.00 $5.00, $0.00 $2.00, $2.00

If you are a row player, your decision buttons will be on the left side of the payoff table, and if you are a column player, your decision buttons will be above the table.

Matchings: At the beginning of each round, there will be a new random pairing of all participants, so the person who you are matched with in one round may not be the same person you will be matched with in the subsequent round. Matchings are random, and you are no more likely to be matched with one person than with another.

Relevant Earnings Round: This session will consist of 3 separate parts, each with more than one round of interactions with another player. However, only one round in each part will be selected at random to determine your earnings for the part. You will not know in advance which round will be used for earnings, although you will be shown provisional earnings results after each round. Then after the final round in each part, you will find out which round was randomly selected to be used, and you will be reminded of the decisions that you and the other player made in that round and what your earnings were.

Total Earnings: Your earnings are determined by the choices that you and the other person make in the randomly determined earnings round for each part. Your total earnings will be the sum of your earnings for each of the 3 relevant rounds, one for each part.

Rounds: There will be 8 rounds in this part of the experiment, with random rematchings at the beginning of each.

  1. Explain why these fractions are interesting, and describe how they change over the five rounds for each game .

Solution: These fractions are interesting because:

  • In Game 1, they correspond to cooperation. People on average did not do this, and seem to converge to the Nash prediction
  • In game 2, they correspond to the higher-payoff (i.e. Payoff-dominant) equilibrium, as opposed to the less risky equilibrium. People seem evenly split between this and the safer action.
  • In Game 3, they correspond to the actions that could yield players the highest payoff. People seem to choose this action more often than not. Looking ahead: What does the mixed strategy Nash equilibrium of this game say about the relative frequencies of B/R vs T/L?
  1. In Game 2, suppose that all the “3” payoffs were changed to ones. Does this change your answer to question 1? Why or why not? Do you think the class would have made different decisions after this change? Explain why.

Game 2 (modified)

Note that the set of Nash equilibria has not changed, but playing T or L guarantees a lower payoff. That is, I need to be less sure that my opponent will play B/R to play B/R myself. In math, I will choose B/R if my beliefs in my opponents strategy \(\sigma=\Pr(B/R)\) satisfies::

\[ \begin{aligned} \text{Game 2:}\quad &4\sigma\geq 3, \quad \sigma \geq \frac{3}{4}\\ \text{Modified Game 2:}\quad 4\sigma &\geq 1, \quad \sigma \geq \frac{1}{4} \end{aligned} \]
